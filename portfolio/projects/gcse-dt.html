// Overview

Designed and manufactured for my GCSE Design & Technology Non-Examined Assessment, this project centred on creating a pull-along toy for young children that went beyond a conventional single-form product. The brief challenged me to produce something that was both engaging and considered in its design — and I wanted to address the issue of toy longevity through a modular approach.

// Design Concept

The core idea was reusability through modularity. Rather than producing a single fixed dinosaur, the toy features interchangeable head and tail pieces that clip onto the main body — allowing a child to reconfigure the toy into different dinosaur species, or mix and match parts to invent entirely new creatures. This extends the life of the product and encourages imaginative play rather than passive interaction.

Interchangeable head and tail pieces — jointed connection system allows parts to be swapped without tools, designed to be robust enough for small children to operate independently.
Solid beech wood body and wheels — selected for its durability, smooth grain, and child-safe finish properties. All edges were shaped and sanded to remove any sharp profiles.
3D printed pull handle — the arched handle was designed in CAD and printed in PLA, allowing a complex curved form that would have been impractical to achieve in wood alone.
Target user: toddlers and young children — proportions, weight, and surface finish were all considered against the needs and safety requirements of the intended age group.
// Manufacture

The body, wheels, and interchangeable pieces were all hand-cut, shaped, and finished in the school workshop. The process involved marking out from technical drawings, cutting on the bandsaw, shaping with chisels and rasps, and finishing through progressive sanding grades. The pull handle was modelled in CAD software and 3D printed separately before being fitted to the body.

Producing accurate joints for the interchangeable pieces required careful measurement and iteration — early prototypes of the connection mechanism were too loose or too tight, and refining the tolerance to a point where a child could operate it easily without the pieces falling off unexpectedly was one of the more demanding aspects of the build.

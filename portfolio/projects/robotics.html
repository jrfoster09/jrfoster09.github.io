// Overview

Student Robotics is a UK-wide competition challenging sixth form teams to design, build, and program a fully autonomous robot from scratch — no remote control, no human input during a match. The 2026 competition was held at the University of Southampton and themed around chemistry, with robots tasked with collecting coloured boxes representing acids (red) and bases (blue).

The scoring system rewarded teams for moving their pH level as far from neutral (pH 7) as possible — collecting an acid and a base would cancel each other out, adding a layer of strategic decision-making to the challenge. Our robot competed against over 20 teams, finishing 4th overall and winning the Robot & Team Image Award.

// My Role
Head Chassis Engineer
Responsible for the physical design and manufacture of the robot's chassis — the structural backbone that everything else mounts to. Led all fabrication work, drawing on my Design & Technology background to produce a chassis that was both competition-legal and mechanically robust under match conditions.

Alongside chassis design, I was responsible for hardware integration — ensuring all electronic components, motors, sensors, and wiring were mounted and routed correctly within the strict rules set out by Student Robotics. This meant cross-referencing the competition rulebook throughout the build process to confirm full compliance before every match.

// The Challenge

Building a robot that operates entirely autonomously presents a different set of engineering problems compared to a manually controlled machine. Every physical decision — wheel placement, centre of gravity, arm geometry — directly affects the robot's ability to sense its environment and execute its software. Working closely with our programming team, I adapted the chassis design iteratively to support the sensors and mechanisms they needed to implement our strategy.

The competition environment itself is unpredictable: lighting varies, other robots interact with yours, and minor manufacturing tolerances compound over the course of a match. Designing for robustness under those conditions was one of the most valuable engineering lessons the project taught me.
